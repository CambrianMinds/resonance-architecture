# Archaeoacoustics: Academic Research Summary

## Peer-Reviewed and Academic Sources on Sound at Ancient Sites

*This document catalogs scholarly research supporting acoustic considerations in ancient construction*

## Key Researchers

### Robert G. Jahn & PEAR (Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research)
- **Institution**: Princeton University
- **Active period**: 1979-2007
- **Key publication**: "Acoustical Resonances of Assorted Ancient Structures" (1996)
- **Methodology**: Systematic acoustic testing of megalithic chambers

**Major finding**: Multiple ancient chambers across the UK and Ireland resonate at approximately **110 Hz** (within male vocal range).

---

### Paul Devereux
- **Focus**: Archaeoacoustics pioneer
- **Approach**: Field measurements at sacred sites worldwide
- **Key work**: Collaborator with PEAR team, extensive individual research

**Key findings**:
- Consistent resonance frequencies across unrelated sites
- Rock art locations correlate with acoustic anomalies
- Ancient sites show evidence of acoustic design consideration

---

### Ian Cook (UCLA)
- **Focus**: Neurological effects of specific frequencies
- **Key study**: EEG measurements at 110 Hz

**Major finding**: 110 Hz produces measurable changes in brain activity, shifting from left-hemisphere (analytical) to right-hemisphere (creative/receptive) dominance.

## Published Research Papers

### "Acoustical Resonances of Assorted Ancient Structures" (1996)
**Authors**: Jahn, Devereux, Ibison  
**Published**: Journal of the Acoustical Society of America (JASA)

**Sites tested**:
- Newgrange (Ireland): **110 Hz**
- Loughcrew (Ireland): **110 Hz**
- Chun Quoit (UK): **113 Hz**
- Wayland's Smithy (UK): **112 Hz**

**Key observations**:
- All tested chambers showed strong resonance in 95-120 Hz range
- Standing wave patterns formed at resonant frequencies
- Chamber dimensions appeared designed for specific frequencies
- Smoke/mist made standing wave patterns visible

---

### "Stone Age Eyes and Ears" (Royal College of Art, 2014)
**Institution**: London Royal College of Art
**Focus**: Preseli bluestones (source of Stonehenge inner circle)

**Key findings**:
- Bluestones are "lithophones" - they ring when struck
- Different stones produce different musical notes
- May have been selected for acoustic properties
- Suggests acoustic function in Stonehenge design

**Quote from BBC coverage**:
> "The study, by London's Royal College of Art, was to try and record what 'Stone Age eyes and ears' would have heard and seen in a prehistoric context."

---

### "Archaeoacoustic Investigation of Maltese Hypogeum" (2008-2014)
**Researchers**: Ruben Zahra, Paolo Debertolis et al.
**Site**: Hypogeum of Hal-Saflieni, Malta

**Key findings**:
- Oracle Chamber resonates at **114 Hz** and **68-70 Hz**
- Male voice can stimulate both frequencies
- Double resonance creates unusual acoustic effects
- Structure amplifies sound dramatically

---

### "Systems of Acoustic Resonance at Ancient Sites and Related Brain Activity" 
**Authors**: Paolo Debertolis et al.
**Published**: Sbresearch Group

**Key methodology**:
- EEG measurements during exposure to resonant frequencies
- Testing at multiple ancient sites
- Correlation with PEAR research findings

**Findings**:
- Consistent brain activity changes at resonant frequencies
- Effects similar to meditation states
- Sites appear designed to induce specific consciousness states

## Neurological Effects Research

### Ian Cook et al. (UCLA)
**Study**: EEG effects of 110 Hz exposure

**Findings**:
- 110 Hz produces shift from left to right hemisphere activity
- Deactivation of language processing center
- Activation of creative/receptive brain regions
- Effects similar to deep meditation

**Implications**:
- Ancient chambers may have been designed for consciousness alteration
- Resonance creates specific neurological states
- Chanting in these spaces would have powerful effects

## Acoustic Properties of Stone

### Preseli Bluestones (Stonehenge)
- Dolerite composition
- Ring when struck (lithophone quality)
- Variable pitches from different stones
- May form "ancient xylophone"

### Granite
- Excellent acoustic transmission
- Low internal damping
- Sustains vibration
- Contains piezoelectric quartz

### Limestone
- Good acoustic shaping
- Higher internal damping
- Often paired with granite for specific effects
