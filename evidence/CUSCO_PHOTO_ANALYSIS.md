## Image 1: Ollantaytambo Polygonal Masonry

### Location
Ollantaytambo, Sacred Valley, Peru - precision andesite masonry

### Key Observations

#### 1. Pillowed/Cushioned Faces
Each stone surface exhibits a characteristic **convex curvature** rather than flat faces:
- Center of each face is "proud" (projects slightly forward)
- Edges where stones meet are slightly recessed relative to face centers
- Creates the distinctive "pillowed" or "cushioned" appearance

**Significance**: This is the predicted signature of vibrational fitting:
- During sustained vibration, maximum contact pressure occurs at face centers
- Abrasive wear removes more material at high-pressure contact points
- Center of faces (lower pressure) retains more material → convex appearance
- This pattern is **emergent** from the process, not designed/carved

#### 2. Complex Polygonal Geometry
- Multiple angles per stone (the famous "12-angle stone" style)
- Each joint is unique - no two interfaces are identical
- Geometry appears to **follow contact patterns**, not a pre-designed template

**Significance**: Template-based fitting would produce regularized geometry. What we see is each joint optimized for its specific contact topology - consistent with in-situ vibrational fitting where the process itself determines the final geometry.

#### 3. Rectangular Recess (Upper Right of Central Stone)
- Appears to be a **nub scar** or deliberately created recess
- Finished surfaces around it (not a break)
- Located on the stone face, not at lifting position

**Significance**: If this is a nub location, its position supports the "vibrational tool anchor" hypothesis rather than the "lifting handle" hypothesis. The nub would have been used during fitting, not transport.

#### 4. Joint Tightness
- Joints are extremely tight despite complex geometry
- No mortar visible
- Famous "razor blade test" - cannot insert blade into joints

**Significance**: This level of precision over complex 3D surfaces is extremely difficult to achieve through any known method. Vibrational fitting would naturally optimize toward maximum contact, explaining both the precision and the consistency.

## Comparative Analysis: What Each Theory Predicts

### If GEOPOLYMER/CASTING:
- Interior surfaces would be **mold-uniform**
- Geometry would be **predictable and regular**
- All surfaces of a single block would show **consistent texture**
- No pillowing on interior surfaces (molds don't pillow)

**What we see**: Irregular interior geometry, pillowing on interior surfaces, directional changes following contact topology.

**Verdict**: ❌ Inconsistent with casting

### If CONVENTIONAL FITTING (Chisel to Template):
- Interior surfaces would be **flat** (that's what chisels produce)
- Corners would be **sharp** (tools create defined edges)
- Surfaces would show **tool marks**
- Fitting stops at a designed geometry

**What we see**: Curved interior surfaces, no visible tool marks, geometry follows contact patterns.

**Verdict**: ❌ Inconsistent with conventional fitting

### If IN-SITU VIBRATIONAL FITTING:
- Interior surfaces would be **curved** (following contact pressure)
- Pillowing would **continue into interior joints**
- Geometry would **change direction** following 3D contact topology
- No tool marks (abrasive action, not cutting)
- Optimization toward maximum contact area

**What we see**: All of the above.

**Verdict**: ✅ Consistent with vibrational fitting

## The Pillowing Signature

### External Faces (Both Images)
- Each stone face shows convex curvature
- Edges at joints are slightly recessed
- Creates distinctive "cushioned" appearance
- Consistent across all visible stones

### Interior Surfaces (Image 2)
- Same pillowing pattern visible on exposed interior face
- Continues into the joint where surfaces met
- Not a feature of external finishing - it's a contact signature

### Mechanism Explanation
During vibrational fitting with abrasive medium:
1. Two stones are brought into approximate contact
2. Vibration is applied (sustained acoustic/mechanical oscillation)
3. Where surfaces actually touch, abrasive action occurs
4. Higher pressure = more material removal
5. Lower pressure (non-contact or light contact) = less removal
6. Process continues until surfaces maximize contact
7. Result: pillowed faces where center contacted less than edges initially

The pillowing IS the process signature.
